The NBA Redrafted 2.0

By Russell Goldenberg | Feb. 2022

Five years ago we redrafted the NBA with data. We figured it was time to update with more recent drafts, and ask some new questions.

So let’s begin. Hmm...I’m guessing you are a fan of the New York Knicks? No shame in admitting it.

Hindsight is 20/20, but it probably stings a bit to see what could have been for the

New York’s actual picks and the better players available.

Where do these better players come from? The short version: each player gets a rating of 0–100 from a blend of four advanced stats based on their top five seasons. The upgrade pick is a player that was drafted lower but has a higher rating. Squads are made up of the biggest rating upgrades. See the method section for details.

The 2010 draft class was perhaps the least exciting (or most accurate), with the most players of any class drafted close to their correct spot. It was also the only class the data suggests had no players that should have dropped 20+ spots.

Data and Methods

Data from Basketball Reference. The four advanced stats used to create a player’s rating were VORP, Win Shares, RAPTOR WAR, and Wins Added. No all-in-one advanced stat is perfect, so I chose a blend that satisfied the eye test (see table below). Each stat was normalized then averaged together to create a master scale of a player’s rating from 0 to 100. Ratings were based on a player’s top five seasons to find the balance between prime and longevity (an average NBA career is 4.5 years).

Only showing draft classes through 2017 because they have at least five years of data. Upgrade choices in the Could Have Been teams were first selected from top 10 picks, then searched beyond those if there weren’t enough to fill the top five. Draft trades were ignored since swaps usually are contingent on player availability and often include multiple players or money.

Report card grades for draft picks were calculated as follows: a player’s potential was the difference between their rating (from above) and the best player available in that draft at their pick. If they were the best player available (i.e., a potential of 0) they got an A+. The remaining grades were evenly distributed, with a uniform number of players receiving each grade value.
